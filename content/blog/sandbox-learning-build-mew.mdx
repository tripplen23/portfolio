## Reality hit

Let me be honest with you. Last two years I was a tourist in my own craft. Bookmarking tutorials, watching conference talks, refreshing threads — all the theater of learning, none of the actual shipping. I knew every framework's landing page by heart and couldn't ship a CLI if my life depended on it.

Then the contract ended. No more company laptop, no more "I'll do it tomorrow." Just me, a bedroom studio, and a hard decision: stop reading, start building.

So I did. Terminal became my whole world. C came back into my life. Rust and Go too — I fell back in love with both after years away. And somewhere in the middle of all that, a project called Mew started growing on its own.

I call the way I learn now **sandbox learning**.

## What is sandbox learning

A sandbox is a place where you can break things on purpose.

Sandbox learning, for me, is:

1. Pick one thing. Not ten.
2. Build a tiny project around it. A real thing, not a TODO app.
3. Read the manual *because* something broke, not before.
4. Keep the box small enough to see the whole thing. No microservices. No premature abstractions.

> *"Learn the rules like a professional. Break them like an artist."*

That's the whole game. Learn the rules by getting your code rejected by a compiler 200 times. Then break them on purpose.

## Information overload — what actually worked

Twitter bookmarks. YouTube "Watch later." Pocket queue. Notion databases. Substack emails I'll never open. The "I'll get to it" garden that turns into a graveyard by Tuesday.

Three moves shut it down:

**1. Killed the microslop OS.** Left Windows. Installed Ubuntu on every laptop I own. Wrote about it in [Why I Don't Play With Microslop OS Anymore](/blogs/why-no-microslop-os-anymore).

**2. Moved into the terminal.** No "Recommended for you" feed. No infinite scroll. Just a prompt and a blinking cursor. Forces you to *intend* every action.

**3. Picked the three things that matter.** Rust. Go. Agent harness engineering. If it's not in those buckets, it doesn't get a tab open.

## What I built to learn

**C and C++.** I wrote a neural net in C++ a while back. Coming back to it after years of higher-level languages felt like coming home. Manual memory. No borrow checker. No GC. Just you, `malloc`, and a `segfault` at 2 AM. Modern languages are kind. C and C++ are honest.

**Rust.** I hadn't touched it in years. Picked it up again and the borrow checker made sense this time. I wrote [Learning Rust in 2026](/blogs/learning-rust-2026) because the muscle memory came back fast once I stopped fighting the language.

**Go.** Doesn't try to impress you. Just does the boring thing competently. Fast builds, concurrency, a single binary you can `scp` to a server. After years of chasing shiny tools, "boring but competent" feels revolutionary.

## A word on AI

I use AI every day. Copilot, ChatGPT, Claude — they're how I move fast.

But learning with AI is learning with a bag of bias strapped to your face. These models invent APIs that don't exist. They give you solutions that "look right" and break in production. If you don't know the fundamentals, you won't even notice you're being lied to.

Two rules:

- Verify everything two ways. Open the real docs. Run the config block. See if it actually does what it claims.
- Know the core cold. Language fundamentals. SDLC. Architecture. The boring stuff is what lets you catch the bullshit.

Sandbox learning with AI is fine. Sandbox learning *only* with AI is a trap — you end up with a codebase you can't defend.

## Then Mew happened

At some point, all three threads converged.

I'd been FOMO-ing about agent harnesses for months. New framework every other week. New protocol. New "this changes everything." I felt productive. I built nothing.

Then one night I just started. Rust workspace. Go daemon. TUI. A name I'd been carrying for years: **Mew**.

[Mew](https://github.com/tripplen23/mew) is an AI agent harness. The real description:

> Mew migrates legacy code, clones features from one product to another, and reconstructs the parts of an application that are painful to write by hand. Inspired by Mew in Pokémon — copy the original, then evolve it into something stronger.

It reads a codebase, understands it, transforms it, and verifies the result behaves the same way it did before. That last part is what made me build [mew-skills](https://github.com/tripplen23/mew-skills) as a separate repo — a spec kit for differential testing and regression scaffolding. Deep dive on that later.

## Why this matters

I started Mew with a CLI flag and a panic. Two months in it has a workspace, a TUI, an MCP layer, differential testing, and a roadmap I can actually see. Every commit is something I understand because I wrote it, broke it, and fixed it.

> Pick one thing. Make it small enough to see. Build it in a sandbox where deleting everything is an option. Stay there until it does something real.

Mew is open source. If you've been wanting to learn by building the same way, I'd genuinely love to collab. A spec kit you can use today lives at [tripplen23/mew-skills](https://github.com/tripplen23/mew-skills). Run it against your own codebase. Improve the regression tests. Send a PR.

I'm pushing as hard as I can to get Mew into everyone's hands. Goal: GODLIKE. We'll see.
